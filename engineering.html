Senior Design

Team picture after the award ceremony

The Shell Eco-Marathon is a competition for high school and college student teams to build ultra-efficient vehicles and compete at the end of the school year. The Mechanical Engineering department at CU Boulder coordinated a team as part of the senior design program to compete in this competition, and I was a part of this team along with six other students (I'm second from the left in the above photo).

Most of the vehicles that achieve the highest fuel efficiency make great use of carbon fiber throughout the vehicle. It is a very lightweight and strong material and is perfect for this application. However, it is also very energy-intensive to manufacture and its use greatly increases the embodied energy of the vehicle.

We wanted to take the "eco" aspect of the competition and run with it by using natural and biodegradable materials wherever we could. These materials, while not as light and rigid, still had impressive mechanical properties, would decrease the embodied energy of our vehicle, and would differentiate ourselves from the crowd.

Testing an NFC sample in the Instron machine

Building on some research done by Dr. Wil Srubar and his group, the natural fiber composite (NFC) material we chose to use was comprised of jute as the fiber (commonly used to make burlap) and a mixture of gelatin, water, and tannins as the resin. There was an incredible amount of experimentation, material testing, and data anlysis required to determine the optimal proportions of water, gelatin, and tannins in the resin and the best coating to protect against water damage.

In the end we were not able to use NFCs in the chassis as the proposed hybrid chassis had weak points where the necessary aluminum tubes met the NFC tubes. However, we were able to successfully mold panels and use them for the vehicle body.
